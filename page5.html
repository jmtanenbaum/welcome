Intellectual statement

The term “Digital Humanities” encompasses multifaceted uses of computational methods to explore humanistic questions and the radical imagination. As Jeffrey Schnapp, Todd Presner, Peter Lunenfeld and Johanna Drucker outline in the Digital Humanities Manifesto, the field initially employed quantitative methods like databases and automation, but soon entered a “second wave” which reemphasized the qualitative, affective, and experimental qualities of the humanities. (Jeffrey Schnapp et al., 2015, p. 2)

I am particularly interested in the Digital Humanities work of Public Historians like Andrew Hurley, who argues that combined with in person outreach and communication, technologies like 3-D modeling can break down divides between past and present and translate historical knowledge into “imagined futures.” (Hurley, 2016, p. 87) The practice of “thick mapping” incorporates multilayered narratives, sources, and contexts that challenge hegemonic cartographic and historical practices. (Presner, 19) The Mapping Indigenous LA project embodies Ariella Azoulay’s praxis of potential history by reconstructing the “concrete events, aspirations, claims, and formations that were crushed by the imposition of the course of history…as always ready and still part of the political space.” (Azoulay, 9) Thick mapping reveals the multilayered and ongoing histories of colonialism, resistance, and survival of the Gabrielino/Tongva and Tataviam people. As D'Ignazio and Klein argue, Data Feminism questions and challenges hegemonic power structures and furthers the struggles of social movements. (D'Ignazio and Klein, 5) I have attempted to reflect this praxis in my own work.

Once the COVID-19 pandemic began, organizers raised the alarm about the deadly state sponsored medical neglect occurring in prisons and jails across the country. I soon began a digital project in February with my colleague Jake Tompkins and funding from a departmental Digital Resources Development Grant to amplify their voices while weaving together incarcerated people’s past and present struggles for liberation. The project included writing, ephemera, art, and film from the incarcerated people’s movements of the 1970s through the present to inspire viewers to join the urgent movement for mass releases during the COVID-19 crisis. I collaborated with the Freedom Archives and the California Coalition for Women Prisoners to create the Rebel Archives in the Golden Gulag digital exhibit. My future goals for the project include producing a zine to make it accessible to incarcerated people, who lack internet access, and recording in person oral histories with more anti-carceral feminist organizers. While I’ve held an interest in critical carceral studies for years, this project sparked my interest in using Digital Humanities tools to tell the stories of social movement history.

I soon enrolled in Miriam Posner’s Digital Humanities class, and then decided to pursue the certificate. My most ambitious project was a Scalar based exhibit I co-curated on behalf of the June L Mazer Lesbian Archives, which contextualizes the history and impact of groundbreaking lesbian glossy magazine Curve in the history of lesbian and queer communities during the 1990s. It includes Tableau based maps and topic models, over 60 digitized records with Dublin Core metadata, and a scalar.js based timeline. I’ve also helped build and share other timely projects with the goal of furthering social struggle, such as a leaflet based thick map revealing the interwoven crises of pollution and mass incarceration in California and a Wax based digital exhibit on the history of housing discrimination in Los Angeles. Finally, I created a dataset, network analysis, and digital exhibit about the relationship between critical theorist Herbert Marcuse and the New Left in the United States. I shared my data on github in the hopes that other scholar-activists will continue to add to it. Ultimately, I share the utopian aspirations of the field, and will continue to attempt to question power and embolden the radical imagination.

Digital Humanities Tools

Content Management Systems

Scalar: "Scalar is a free, open source authoring and publishing platform that’s designed to make it easy for authors to write long-form, born-digital scholarship online. Scalar enables users to assemble media from multiple sources and juxtapose them with their own writing in a variety of ways, with minimal technical expertise required." Scalar is excellent because it includes the ability to create data visualizations, timelines, and maps and a gorgeous user interface. 

Omeka: "Omeka provides open-source web publishing platforms for sharing digital collections and creating media-rich online exhibits."

Web Maps

Tableau Public: Tableau is a powerful data visualization tool that can also easily create interactive maps. It is great for beginners. 

Leaflet: Leaflet is an open source javascript library for web mapping that works well with other libraries. "Leaflet is designed with simplicity, performance and usability in mind. It works efficiently across all major desktop and mobile platforms, can be extended with lots of plugins, has a beautiful, easy to use and well-documented API and a simple, readable source code that is a joy to contribute to."

Recognito: "Work on texts and images. Identify and mark named entities. Use your data in other tools or connect to other data on the Web. Without the need to learn code." 

Data Management

Open Refine: "OpenRefine (previously Google Refine) is a powerful tool for working with messy data: cleaning it; transforming it from one format into another; and extending it with web services and external data."

Network Analysis

Cytoscape: "Cytoscape is an open source software platform for visualizing complex networks and integrating these with any type of attribute data. A lot of Apps are available for various kinds of problem domains, including bioinformatics, social network analysis, and semantic web." 

Gephi: "Gephi is the leading visualization and exploration software for all kinds of graphs and networks. Gephi is open-source and free." Gephi is gorgeous to look at but requires a high powered computer to use. 

Primary Source Research

Trophy: "Take control of your research photos with Tropy, a tool that shortens the path from finding archival sources to writing about them. Spend more time using your research photos, and less time searching for them."
